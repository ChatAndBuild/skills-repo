# History Knowledge — reference

Use this file when the user needs **deeper methodology**, **source literacy**, or **frameworks** beyond the main `SKILL.md` checklist.

## Source criticism (quick)

- **Provenance** — Where did the artifact come from, and what chain of custody is known?
- **Genre** — Law, propaganda, private letter, chronicle, archaeology, oral tradition: each has typical distortions.
- **Audience and purpose** — Praise, blame, legitimation, and silence are all data about intent, not transparent windows on events.
- **Corroboration** — Independent lines (material, documentary, linguistic, environmental) strengthen claims; single-source dramatic stories need caution.
- **Silences** — Who is missing from the archive (illiterate, colonized, enslaved, women in many societies)? Absence of evidence ≠ evidence of absence.

## Primary vs secondary (working definitions)

- **Primary** — Contemporary to the period under study (or physical traces from it): inscriptions, charters, coins, excavation layers, newspapers from the day, transcripts of speeches when authentic.
- **Secondary** — Later analysis: monographs, peer-reviewed articles, museum catalogs that synthesize evidence. Textbooks are secondary and often simplified.

## Layered causation (template)

When explaining “why,” stack at least three timescales when relevant:

1. **Long arc** — Structures: geography, demography, institutions, recurring economic patterns.
2. **Medium term** — Shifting coalitions, reforms, ideologies, military or fiscal buildup.
3. **Trigger** — A decision, crisis, assassination, drought, or spark event that starts the clock on a visible change.

Name **one alternative framing** (e.g., “economic historians stress…, while cultural historians emphasize…”) when debates are live.

## Periodization and labels

- Period names (**Renaissance**, **Dark Ages**, **Axial Age**) are **interpretive**; say who uses them and why others reject them.
- **Calendar systems** — BCE/CE vs BC/AD is stylistic; be consistent in one answer. Note Julian/Gregorian shifts when they affect dating precision.
- **National frames** — Modern nation-states projected backward is a common error; use polity names accurate to the era when possible.

## Schools and angles (one-line each)

Historians often mix these; labels help the user see **why two books disagree**.

- **Political / diplomatic** — States, treaties, elites, wars.
- **Social** — Classes, labor, family, everyday life.
- **Cultural / intellectual** — Ideas, religion, art, memory.
- **Economic** — Trade, agriculture, labor systems, inequality.
- **Postcolonial / subaltern** — Power in archives, empire, resistance, whose voice counts.
- **Environmental** — Climate, disease, resource shocks as constraints and catalysts.

## Controversy and ethics

- **Genocide, slavery, war crimes** — Use precise legal and historical terms; do not minimize documented harm; distinguish legal verdicts from ongoing historical debate over numbers or intent where evidence is contested.
- **Living memory** — Recent events may have political stakes; separate **reported facts**, **interpretation**, and **normative judgment** when the user asks “what happened” vs “was it justified.”

## Answering “how do we know?”

Give a **source map**: “We mainly know X from [A]; Y is inferred from [B]; Z is still disputed because [C].” Offer **what would change the picture** (new dig, archive opening, DNA, tree rings).

## Further reading (generic guidance)

Prefer **recent scholarly surveys** or **museum/university** over random blogs for unfamiliar topics. When the user wants depth, suggest **one monograph + one primary-source anthology** style pairing rather than a long list.
